Why we need a new leaderboard now

The vector database market has moved past a simple "who has the highest QPS?" phase. Most production teams now choose managed vector database products under budget, latency, freshness, and operational constraints. In that world, cost often dominates raw throughput: a faster system is not the better choice if it needs meaningfully more monthly spend to satisfy the same workload.

VDBBench Leaderboard v1 still matters. It gives a clean way to compare raw engine behavior across open-source and cloud systems. But it does not fully answer the questions teams ask when they are about to operate a cloud vector database in production. When does newly inserted data become searchable? When is it fully indexed? What happens when a query returns metadata or vectors, not just IDs? What happens when the workload is split across many tenants? And what does each QPS tier actually cost?

CloudLeadboard v2 is designed around those questions. It keeps performance in view, but puts it next to the production behaviors and cost model that determine what a customer can actually deploy.

What CloudLeadboard v2 changes

CloudLeadboard v2 is not a replacement for the v1 raw-performance board. It is a new decision layer for production cloud evaluation. The goal is to make the leaderboard useful for a buyer who is deciding between real managed products, not only for a reader comparing peak benchmark numbers.

Dimension CloudLeadboard v1 CloudLeadboard v2
Main question How fast can this vector database go under benchmark conditions? Which managed cloud product best fits a production workload and budget?
Primary lens Raw search performance: QPS, latency, and recall. Production behavior: readiness, payload sensitivity, tenancy, cold latency, and cost.
Product scope Broad comparison across open-source and cloud vector databases. Focused comparison of production-ready managed cloud offerings.
Insert story Mostly treats loaded data as ready for search benchmarking. Measures the path from insert completion to fully searchable and fully indexed.
Search response Primarily evaluates search result quality and speed. Separates IDs-only responses from metadata and raw-vector payload responses.
Tenant model Usually reads as a single-dataset, single-namespace benchmark. Adds multi-tenant workload shape with Cohere 10M split across 1,000 tenants.
Cold behavior Warm benchmark loops can hide first-query latency. Reports cold latency, warm latency, and cold/warm ratio directly.
Cost model Cost is secondary to performance ranking. Cost is a first-class result through QPS curves, Pareto points, and crossovers.
How to read it Use it to understand engine capability and raw performance envelopes. Use it to choose the product and configuration that fits a real traffic tier.

This matters because a single top-line QPS ranking can hide important tradeoffs. A product can look strong on peak throughput while losing ground on recall, tail latency, cold-start behavior, or cost at sustained production QPS. Conversely, a lower-cost profile may be exactly right when the workload can tolerate its storage or cold-latency characteristics.

The new tests we added

Version 2 introduces cloud-oriented cases in VectorDBBench. Each case is designed to catch a production behavior that a plain QPS leaderboard can miss. The excerpts below are intentionally selective: they show the shape of the finding, while the full result repository remains the source for complete matrices and reproduction details.

CloudInsertCase: when writes are actually ready

CloudInsertCase separates client-side insert completion from the states users actually care about: searchable and indexed. That makes insert a two-part question: how fast can a service accept the rows, and how long until those rows are safe to search with indexed performance?

Batch size changes two different numbers

On LAION 100M, larger batches show turbopuffer's best ingest strength clearly. With backpressure disabled, it reaches 14.3K rows/s at batch 5k and 15.8K rows/s at batch 10k, ahead of the Zilliz rows on raw acceptance rate. That is useful for teams optimizing a one-way upload path.

The ready-time view is the more production-oriented signal. Zilliz Cloud Capacity 12CU finishes the searchable-and-indexed path in about 3.1-3.2 hours across the tested batch sizes, while Zilliz Tiered 4CU stays around 4.2-4.3 hours. turbopuffer improves sharply as batch size grows, but its fastest ingest mode still lands at 8.16 hours in the batch-10k run.

Pinecone Serverless is included now as a baseline from the ready data. Its insert rate is much lower in this excerpt: 249 rows/s at batch 1k, 389 rows/s at batch 5k, and 384 rows/s at batch 10k, which stretches total ready time to roughly 72-112 hours. One notable detail is that once Pinecone accepts the rows, the additional searchable and indexed wait is effectively zero in these runs. A reasonable interpretation is that Pinecone may be intentionally limiting write intake while a more complex serverless indexing path builds and optimizes work under the hood. That makes it a different tradeoff from Zilliz and turbopuffer rather than just a slower line in the table.

The business read is straightforward. Zilliz is the cleaner fit when fresh embeddings must become searchable quickly, such as a RAG knowledge-base rebuild, marketplace catalog refresh, security corpus reindex, or daily multimodal content ingestion job. turbopuffer is attractive when the priority is getting a very large batch accepted quickly and the application can tolerate a longer ready window, for example offline backfills, archival namespaces, or preproduction data staging. Pinecone's model fits teams that value serverless operations and slower, steady ingestion over aggressive bulk refresh, especially when the AI application has modest update volume.

Cost changes the insert decision

Ingestion is also a cost event. A system that accepts rows quickly can still be expensive for a backfill, migration, or recurring refresh. Zilliz Cloud Tiered and Capacity use a dedicated cluster model, so insert cost is mainly the hourly cluster charge spread across the full load. In the LAION 100M cost model, that puts the one-time load around $20.31 for Zilliz Tiered 8CU and $20.87 for Zilliz Capacity 16CU, with Capacity 12CU projected at no more than $18.60.

turbopuffer and Pinecone are closer to a pay-as-you-go serverless model. That is convenient and can be less expensive for customers with light or infrequent search demand, but writing and maintaining a decently sized namespace still carries a material usage-metered cost. In this excerpt, turbopuffer's batch-10k backpressure-off bulk load is modeled at $308.00, while Pinecone Serverless is estimated at about $1.18K from write-unit pricing. The point of this view is to show both sides: serverless lowers operational friction, while dedicated capacity can become much more cost efficient when the workload is large enough and predictable enough.

For buyers, this mostly separates always-on AI systems from occasional-use systems. A production RAG service, recommendation index, or agent-memory platform that refreshes data frequently benefits from spreading write work over dedicated Zilliz capacity. Serverless products remain appealing when traffic is spiky, teams want less capacity planning, or the namespace is queried rarely, but the write bill needs to be reviewed before assuming serverless is cheaper end to end.

CloudPayloadSearchCase: search with real response bodies

Production search often returns more than IDs. CloudPayloadSearchCase measures what happens when the result body includes only IDs, scalar metadata, or full raw vectors, then repeats the same idea under integer and scalar-label filters. That is important because topK 100 vector payloads are much heavier than ID-only results: roughly 309 KB/query in this matrix, compared with about 2 KB/query for IDs-only.

Payload size changes the unfiltered ranking

turbopuffer deserves credit on the unfiltered path. In the vector-payload row, unpinned turbopuffer reaches 382.2 QPS, ahead of Zilliz Capacity 12CU at 229.4 QPS. It also stays around 395-400 QPS for unfiltered IDs-only and scalar-label returns. That is a real strength when the workload is mostly broad, unfiltered retrieval and raw QPS is the primary target.

Zilliz Capacity is more balanced across quality and payload shape. It reaches 376.0 QPS for IDs-only, 379.5 QPS for scalar-label payloads, and 229.4 QPS for vector payloads, while keeping unfiltered recall at 0.9723. Zilliz Tiered 4CU is naturally smaller at roughly 44-50 QPS, but it keeps a clear role as lower-cost managed capacity when the workload does not require the larger Capacity profile.

Pinecone Serverless provides a useful cautionary baseline. In the current payload-search data it was measured at low paced concurrency because higher unpaced concurrency produced rate-limit responses. Its unfiltered rows sit around 4.5 QPS for IDs, scalar labels, and vectors, with recall around 0.961. That makes Pinecone easy to include as a serverless reference point, but not competitive in this particular LAION 100M payload run.

For AI applications, the unfiltered result matters most for broad semantic retrieval: image search, media discovery, candidate generation, and general RAG fetches where the app asks for nearest neighbors first and filters later. turbopuffer is strongest when that workflow is dominated by broad fanout and raw response throughput. Zilliz Capacity is a better fit when the application needs the returned vectors or metadata but also needs stronger recall and predictable managed behavior. Pinecone remains a low-ops option for smaller or less latency-sensitive serverless retrieval paths, but the current numbers do not position it as the high-throughput choice here.

Filters change the story again

The Zilliz advantage shows up most clearly when filters are part of the request. At the 99.9% integer-filter/vector point, Zilliz Tiered 4CU reaches 955.7 QPS and Zilliz Capacity 12CU reaches 933.0 QPS. The same row puts turbopuffer unpinned at 45.1 QPS with roughly 7s p99, while Pinecone Serverless is around 4.8 QPS with recall still pending for the filtered rows. Payload and filter shape can therefore flip the ranking completely.

That filtered shape maps directly to production AI workloads: per-user memory search, permissioned enterprise RAG, catalog search with category and region constraints, or retrieval over embeddings plus business metadata. In those contexts, Zilliz has the clearer story because the benchmark shows high filtered QPS with low p99 latency. turbopuffer still has a place when the metadata constraints are light or the workload is mostly unfiltered, but the selective-filter rows show why v2 cannot stop at a single unfiltered QPS number.

CloudMultitenantSearchCase: SaaS-shaped traffic

CloudMultitenantSearchCase splits Cohere 10M across 1,000 tenants, assigns vectors by tenant, and sends each query to a random tenant. That changes the benchmark from "how fast can one large collection search" to "how well does the system behave when traffic is fragmented across many customer spaces." For SaaS AI products, that is often the real shape: customer workspaces, per-account memory, project-level RAG, support copilots, and agent memory stores rarely search one global namespace without tenant boundaries.

Tenant isolation changes the benchmark

The current matched matrix gives turbopuffer its clearest raw-throughput win. With namespaces aligned to tenants, turbopuffer reaches 3,855 QPS on unfiltered IDs-only search, 3,835 QPS on the 99.9% integer-filter/vector row, and commonly sits in the 3.9K-4.6K QPS band for IDs-only or scalar-label payloads. That is a meaningful strength for products where each tenant is naturally small, independent, and serverless-friendly.

Zilliz Cloud Capacity 2CU is still the stronger Zilliz profile and stays much more usable than Tiered for vector payloads. It reaches 889 QPS on unfiltered IDs-only search, 371 QPS when unfiltered queries return vectors, and 1,306 QPS on the highly selective integer-filter/vector excerpt. Tiered 1CU is more constrained on heavy vector returns, but it remains relevant for smaller tenants, lighter retrieval bodies, and teams trying to keep managed capacity costs low.

Where each product fits

For AI SaaS with many lightweight tenants, turbopuffer has the cleanest story in this case: namespace-per-tenant routing, strong aggregate throughput, and low observed latency on the matched rows. That can be attractive for workspace search, per-customer chatbot memory, or a multi-tenant support assistant where most customers have modest data and the product wants the operational simplicity of serverless scaling.

Zilliz Capacity fits a different operating posture. It is more compelling when tenants are larger, more valuable, or more regulated, and when the buyer wants provisioned capacity, predictable behavior, recall governance, and a cost envelope that can be reasoned about ahead of time. In practical terms, that means enterprise RAG deployments, permissioned knowledge bases, internal copilots, or agent platforms where a few high-value tenants may dominate usage and the team would rather reserve stable capacity than let every namespace scale independently.

Pinecone Serverless is included as a detached serverless reference, not a like-for-like concurrency comparison. Its multitenant run was collected at concurrency 4 rather than the Zilliz/turbopuffer 60/80 matrix, but the numbers are still useful as a low-ops baseline: most measured rows sit around 500-600 QPS. That makes Pinecone relevant for teams that prioritize managed serverless ergonomics and moderate tenant traffic, while this v2 case gives Zilliz and turbopuffer more room to show the tradeoff between provisioned governance and namespace-shaped throughput.

CloudColdLatencyCase: the first query after idle

Cold latency is easy to hide in warm benchmark loops, but users feel it immediately. In the LAION 100M ids-only cold-latency run, Zilliz Capacity 12CU has the cleanest accepted cold-tail result: 22.8 ms cold p99 and 11.5 ms cold average. Unpinned turbopuffer shows a much larger cold-start profile, including a 2.05s first cold query.

turbopuffer's pinned two-replica mode is worth calling out, because it materially improves the result: 58.3 ms cold p99 and a 64.2 ms first cold query. That is a meaningful strength for customers willing to operate a pinned mode. Zilliz Capacity still has the lower cold tail in this excerpt, while Zilliz Tiered is marked for rebench because its cold tail is much higher than warm.

Cold latency is a business problem for AI features that users touch sporadically: an internal knowledge assistant opened a few times per day, a long-tail customer workspace, an on-demand document analysis flow, or an agent that wakes up after idle time. Zilliz Capacity is strongest when the first query needs to feel interactive without special operating modes. turbopuffer pinned mode is a credible answer when a team wants the serverless product but is willing to pay for warmer replicas on latency-sensitive namespaces. Default unpinned behavior is better suited to background, batch, or less interactive retrieval paths where a cold miss is acceptable.

The cost view that actually helps buyers

The Pareto cost board is the most important new decision surface in CloudLeadboard v2. It shows the estimated cost of each product at different target QPS levels and marks the products that are Pareto-optimal under the benchmark's latency, recall, payload, and readiness constraints.

In the current LAION 100M compute model, turbopuffer is compelling for very low sustained QPS. That is a legitimate strength of pay-per-query serverless pricing. But once traffic crosses the low single-digit QPS range, Zilliz Cloud's flat CU-hour economics become the stronger cost story. The current crossover is at 8 QPS: from that point onward, the cheapest reachable Zilliz option is less expensive than turbopuffer in this model.

This is exactly why v2 needs a cost board. A customer should not have to infer the buying decision from a raw QPS table. The board should say where turbopuffer's low-traffic advantage applies, where Zilliz Tiered becomes the more economical active-production option, and where Zilliz Capacity is the better fit for higher-QPS or latency-sensitive serving.

What to keep in mind

This is a release note, not the full benchmark report. The job here is to introduce the new leaderboard, explain why the cases matter, and make the direction of the current findings clear. Detailed tables, reproduction commands, raw JSON paths, and edge-case analysis belong in the benchmark report and source artifact repository.

  • Pricing changes over time, so every cost chart needs the pricing date and region.
  • Vendor configuration choices can change results, especially for managed products with scaling controls or serverless behavior.
  • Fully indexed status may be exposed differently by product; the report must document how it is detected or inferred.
  • The current multi-tenant workload uses Cohere 10M split across 1,000 tenants; it is useful, but it does not represent every tenant distribution.
  • Cold latency depends on cache state, idle windows, and storage architecture, so the test definition must remain strict.
  • Cost Pareto winners are workload-specific and should always be read together with latency, recall, and payload constraints.
